# Starting as a Software Engineer at Legna Software

*May 1, 2026*

---

After graduating from Radford University in May 2025 with a 4.0 GPA, I landed my first full-time software engineering role at [Legna Software](https://www.legnasoftware.com) — a small, focused company building procurement tools for the wood products industry. This post is a reflection on my first weeks in the role and the lessons that came with them.

## The Product: Angel

The product I work on is called [Angel](https://www.legnasoftware.com/angel). It's a cloud-based platform that streamlines lumber and wood product procurement.

It's a real production system with real customers. That's a different experience than anything I built in school, and the learning curve was immediately apparent.

## Jumping Into a Real Codebase

The first week was humbling. I was handed a codebase I didn't write, with patterns I hadn't seen before, and expected to start contributing quickly. A few things helped:

- **Reading before writing.** I resisted the urge to change things immediately and spent time understanding *why* code was written the way it was.
- **Asking questions.** There's no prize for pretending you understand something you don't. Asking early saves hours of going in the wrong direction.
- **Small PRs.** My first contributions were small and focused. Getting something merged — even something minor — builds momentum and trust.
- **Using AI to my advantage.** AI has made massive jumps, and it can interpret code much faster any human can. Using it to explain, find, and write code has increased my rate of learning exponentially.

## What Surprised Me

**How much communication matters.** In school, I wrote code alone or in small groups with clear specs. At work, requirements are sometimes ambiguous, priorities shift, and a lot of my job is clarifying *what* to build before figuring out *how* to build it.

**How often the right answer is "keep it simple."** There were moments where I started reaching for a clever solution and my instinct was wrong. There's a reason *KISS* (Keep It Simple Stupid) is such a popular principle.

## Looking Ahead

I'm excited about where Angel is heading. There's meaningful work to do across the stack, and the team is genuinely invested in building something great. I'm learning faster than I ever did in a classroom.

If you're early in your career and wondering whether school prepared you — it did, but not in the way you might expect. The technical skills are a foundation. The real education starts on day one of the job. My manager often reminds me during our 1-1s that my job for the foreseeable future is to simply absorb as much knowledge as possible.
